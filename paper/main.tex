\begin{abstract}
Reported success rates for pretrained LeRobot~\cite{cadene2024lerobot}
policies live in self-reported tables across heterogeneous evaluation
protocols, with no shared seed accounting or confidence intervals. We
introduce \emph{lerobot-bench}, a public reproducible evaluation suite
that runs a fixed roster of six policies --- four pretrained learned
policies (Diffusion Policy, ACT, SmolVLA, XVLA) and two weights-free
floor baselines (no-op, uniform-random) --- on six LeRobot sim envs
(PushT, Aloha-transfer-cube, and the four LIBERO task
suites~\cite{liu2023libero}), 22~runnable \textit{(policy, env)} cells
after the checkpoint-availability filter. Each cell is evaluated under
a multi-seed contract (5~seeds $\times$ 50~episodes, $N{=}250$ binary
outcomes), and we report Wilson and bootstrap 95\% confidence
intervals on every cell plus paired comparisons across cells with
explicit minimum-detectable-effect (MDE) bounds. Every policy entry is
pinned to a fixed git revision SHA at lock-in time, so a parquet row
is replayable bit-for-bit by anyone with the same lerobot version. The
benchmark, the interactive Hugging Face Space, and the analysis
notebook that produced every figure here are released as open data.
SmolVLA on LIBERO-10 measures $\hat{p}{=}0.252$ [0.202, 0.309] under
the lerobot-bench v1 default protocol, against the 0.71 reported by
Shukor et al.~\cite{shukor2025smolvla} --- but a v1.0.1 methodology
audit (PRs~\#84, \#86, \#89) has since established that this gap
is bounded by two scope mismatches with the paper protocol and is
\emph{not} a clean replication number: (i) the paper averages
10~tasks $\times$ 10~trials per suite, whereas we ran
\texttt{task\_id=0} $\times$ 5~seeds $\times$ 50~episodes; and
(ii) 74.8\% of our failed \texttt{libero\_10} episodes hit the 520-step
cap, whereas canonical LIBERO uses 600~steps for every suite, so
0.252 is a lower bound on what the same checkpoint would score
under the paper's termination rule. The number is real for the
single-task-at-truncated-cap scope; the audit constrains what it
means, not whether the measurement happened. The broader implication
is that single-seed self-reported numbers from individual policy
papers should not be compared head-to-head without re-running
under a shared protocol --- and the protocol we ship is one such.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

LeRobot~\cite{cadene2024lerobot} ships pretrained checkpoints for
Diffusion Policy~\cite{chi2023diffusion}, ACT~\cite{zhao2023learning},
SmolVLA~\cite{shukor2025smolvla}, XVLA~\cite{bu2025xvla}, and the
$\pi_0$ family~\cite{black2024pi0} across simulated manipulation envs
(PushT, Aloha, LIBERO). Individual policy papers report success rates
on these envs, but the protocols differ in seed counts, episode
budgets, success thresholds, and how confidence intervals (if any) are
derived. The result is a set of numbers in PDFs that cannot be trusted
head-to-head --- a problem documented repeatedly in adjacent
fields~\cite{henderson2018deep,agarwal2021deep}.

We fill that gap with \emph{lerobot-bench}. The artifact has three
release surfaces: open data (a public Hub dataset
\texttt{thrmnn/lerobot-bench-v1} of every per-episode outcome
plus an MP4 of every rollout, indexed by \textit{(policy, env, seed,
episode)}); open eval (a pinned, scriptable evaluation pipeline that
re-derives any single parquet row in $<1$\,h on commodity hardware,
plus the resumable orchestrator that produced the full sweep); and
open analysis (the notebook \texttt{notebooks/01-write-finding.ipynb}
that produces every table and figure in this paper, including the
bootstrap CIs, the paired-comparison effect sizes, and the
failure-mode taxonomy).

The v1 roster is four learned policies (Diffusion Policy, ACT,
SmolVLA, XVLA) and two weights-free baselines (no-op, uniform-random),
crossed with six envs. After filtering \textit{(policy, env)} cells to
those with a public Hub checkpoint, the matrix has 22~runnable cells.
The $\pi_0$ family is deferred to v1.1 for a host-RAM reason detailed
in the Limitations.

The contribution is methodological as much as empirical. We document
the seeding contract that makes per-cell numbers bit-reproducible at
cell boundaries, the bootstrap protocol that makes the CIs defensible
at $N{=}250$ binary outcomes per cell, and the
minimum-detectable-effect bound that prevents over-claiming on small
deltas. The benchmark itself is the data; this paper is the defense.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Robot-learning benchmarks.}
PushT~\cite{chi2023diffusion} and the Aloha simulation
suite~\cite{zhao2023learning} were each introduced as evaluation
harnesses for a single policy class --- diffusion-based visuomotor
control and bimanual action chunking respectively --- and the success
rates published in those papers remain the de facto reference. LIBERO
\cite{liu2023libero} was designed as a benchmark from the outset, with
four task suites (\textit{spatial}, \textit{object}, \textit{goal},
\textit{long}; the latter is exposed in lerobot as
\texttt{libero\_10}) targeting compositional generalisation.
RLBench~\cite{james2020rlbench} and CALVIN~\cite{mees2022calvin}
provide larger task taxonomies but do not currently host pretrained
LeRobot-compatible checkpoints; the Open X-Embodiment
collaboration~\cite{bohg2025open} provides cross-embodiment training
data but does not prescribe an evaluation protocol on these specific
envs. None of the above ship a multi-policy leaderboard with shared
seed accounting, multi-seed CIs, and re-runnable rows for the
LeRobot policy stack --- which is the gap lerobot-bench fills.

\paragraph{Statistical rigor in policy evaluation.}
Henderson et al.~\cite{henderson2018deep} showed that single-seed deep
RL claims are largely irreproducible across seeds; Agarwal et
al.~\cite{agarwal2021deep} formalised the prescription for multi-seed
reporting with stratified bootstrap CIs and per-task distributional
summaries on Atari and DeepMind Control. We adopt the same statistical
posture --- multi-seed evaluation, bootstrap CIs, paired comparisons,
and MDE bounds --- for the LeRobot manipulation matrix, where no
comparable protocol has been published and the single-seed
self-reported numbers in individual policy papers presently fill that
role.

\paragraph{Floor baselines.}
A leaderboard without a no-op and a uniform-random baseline cannot
separate ``the env is hard'' from ``the policy is good'': some LeRobot
envs admit non-zero success under random action --- PushT in
particular has a coverage-style reward whose expected value under
uniform action is empirically positive. We therefore evaluate two
weights-free baselines on every cell of the matrix as a leaderboard
floor, mirroring the convention in the OpenAI
Gym~\cite{brockman2016openai} ecosystem from which the LeRobot env API
descends.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\paragraph{Sweep contract.}
The unit of evaluation is a \textit{cell}: a tuple
$(\text{policy}, \text{env}, \text{seed\_idx})$ with
$\text{seed\_idx} \in \{0,1,2,3,4\}$. Each cell runs $n_\text{ep}$
episodes; the contract is $n_\text{ep}{=}50$ unless the auto-downscope
rule (below) reduces it for a slow VLA. The total per-cell sample
size at the contract is $N = 5 \cdot 50 = 250$ binary outcomes. An
episode runs from \texttt{env.reset(seed=$s$)} until
\texttt{terminated=True}, \texttt{truncated=True}, or step count
equals env-specific \texttt{max\_steps} (PushT 300, Aloha 400, LIBERO
$\in\{280,280,300,520\}$ for spatial/object/goal/10 --- inherited
from lerobot defaults; the canonical LIBERO protocol uses
\texttt{max\_steps=600} for all four suites, flagged as a v1.0.1
audit caveat in Section~\ref{sec:results-audit}). Success is the
per-env standard reward threshold from
\texttt{lerobot.envs.<env>.config.SUCCESS\_REWARD}; the episode
succeeds iff its final-step reward meets or exceeds it. PR~\#90
adds a selectable \texttt{--canonical} criterion that swaps in the
paper-canonical termination rule and 600-step LIBERO cap; v1 ships
the lerobot-default rule, and v1.1 reruns affected cells under the
canonical option (Section~\ref{sec:results-audit}).

\paragraph{Seeding contract.}
Per cell, three RNGs are seeded with the same value:
$\text{seed}_\text{cell} = \text{seed\_idx} \cdot 1000$
(\texttt{numpy.random.seed}, \texttt{torch.manual\_seed},
\texttt{torch.cuda.manual\_seed\_all}). Per episode, the env is reset
with $\text{seed}_\text{ep}(e) = \text{seed}_\text{cell} + e$ for
$e \in \{0,\ldots,n_\text{ep}{-}1\}$. Policy stochasticity inherits
the torch generator and is \emph{not} re-seeded per episode. The
torch generator therefore advances across episodes within a cell,
which means mid-cell resume is \emph{not} bit-reproducible: a cell
that crashes mid-way must restart from $e{=}0$. The orchestrator
(\texttt{checkpointing.plan\_resume}) classifies cells as completed,
partial, or pending, and re-runs partial cells from scratch.

\paragraph{Per-cell confidence intervals.}
For each cell we report $\hat{p} = (\text{successes})/N$ with two CIs
that we sanity-check against each other: (i)~the closed-form Wilson
score interval~\cite{wilson1927probable}, recommended for proportions
near zero or one~\cite{agresti1998approximate}; and (ii)~a
percentile-bootstrap 95\% interval~\cite{efron1979bootstrap,
efron1993introduction} with $n_\text{resamples}{=}10{,}000$ resamples
drawn with a seeded \texttt{numpy.random.Generator(42)}. The unit of
resampling is the \emph{episode}, not the seed: bootstrapping over
the 5 seeds gives wide CIs and conflates between-seed variance with
the inferential question. The two intervals agree to within 1\,pp at
$N{=}250$ across the entire $\hat{p}$ grid; we display Wilson in the
leaderboard table for reproducibility (closed-form, no RNG) and
bootstrap in the forest plot for visual symmetry.

\paragraph{Paired comparisons across cells.}
For pairs of cells with identical $(\text{seed\_idx}, e)$ pairings ---
i.e.~the same protocol on the same env --- we report a paired
$\Delta\hat{p}$ via two methods. First, a paired bootstrap
(\texttt{stats.paired\_delta\_bootstrap}, same $n_\text{resamples}$
and seed as the marginal CI), giving a pivotal CI on the
difference~\cite{efron1993introduction}. Second, the two-sided
Wilcoxon signed-rank test~\cite{wilcoxon1945individual} on per-episode
outcome differences (\texttt{stats.paired\_wilcoxon},
\texttt{zero\_method=`wilcox'} drops zero-difference pairs as is
standard for binary paired data; for binary outcomes this reduces in
the limit to McNemar's test~\cite{mcnemar1947note}). Effect size is
Cohen's $h$~\cite{cohen1988statistical}:
$h = 2\arcsin(\sqrt{p_1}) - 2\arcsin(\sqrt{p_2})$,
with conventional bands $|h|<0.2$ small, $<0.5$ medium, $\geq 0.8$
large. When the headline table reports more than one ranking claim
per env, the family-wise rate is controlled by Bonferroni
correction~\cite{bonferroni1936teoria} at $\alpha{=}0.05$ over the
$k$ pairwise comparisons within that env.

\paragraph{Minimum detectable effect.}
At $N{=}250$, the Wilson 95\% half-width at the worst case
$\hat{p}{=}0.5$ is $\text{HW} = 0.0615$
(\texttt{stats.wilson\_ci(125,250)}; full $p$-grid in
\texttt{docs/MDE\_TABLE.md}). Any
$|\Delta\hat{p}| < 2\cdot\text{HW} = 0.1230$ is inconclusive at this
$N$ regardless of the bootstrap or Wilcoxon $p$-value: two cells
whose marginal CIs each have half-width up to 0.0615 can produce a
sample delta below 0.1230 from sampling noise alone. The threshold
shrinks for saturated and near-zero cells (e.g.\ $0.0543$ at
$\hat{p}{=}0.95$); \texttt{docs/MDE\_TABLE.md} tabulates the per-$\hat{p}$
threshold and the empirical paired bootstrap MDE at
$\rho \in \{0,0.3,0.5\}$. Every paired comparison in
Section~\ref{sec:results} is annotated with whether its delta clears
the per-cell threshold; deltas below it are reported as
\emph{inconclusive at $N{=}250$} rather than as findings.

\paragraph{Auto-downscope rule.}
\label{sec:downscope}
Total per-cell wall-clock budget is 3~hours on a single RTX~4060
laptop GPU (8\,GB VRAM). After Day-0 calibration produces a per-policy
mean step latency $\overline{t}$ (ms/step), the per-cell
episodes-per-seed count is
\begin{equation}
    n_\text{ep} = \min\!\left(50,\ \left\lfloor
        \frac{3 \cdot 3600}{5 \cdot \texttt{env.max\_steps} \cdot
        \overline{t} / 1000} \right\rfloor\right).
\end{equation}
The seed count is held fixed at five; only $n_\text{ep}$ shrinks, so a
down-scoped cell lands at $N \in \{50, 100\}$ rather than the
contracted $N{=}250$, with the wider CI flagged in the leaderboard
caption (per-$N$ half-widths in \texttt{docs/MDE\_TABLE.md}\,\S 3). Two
cells in v1 were down-scoped after calibration flagged slow inference.
If a policy's per-cell minimum (5~seeds $\times$ 10~episodes
$=N{=}50$) still exceeds budget, the policy is dropped from v1, not
silently truncated.

\paragraph{Sparse-matrix policy.}
A \textit{(policy, env)} cell is N/A only when no public Hub
checkpoint exists at lock-in time; the 6-policy $\times$ 6-env grid
therefore reduces to 22~runnable cells. Sparsity is reported as a
visible gap in Table~\ref{tab:leaderboard}, not silently skipped, and
cross-policy comparisons are made only within shared envs.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
Each parquet row carries the code commit SHA, the lerobot version
pin (\texttt{lerobot==0.5.1}), and the SHA-256 of its rendered MP4.
Every entry in \texttt{configs/policies.yaml} is locked to a fixed
\texttt{revision\_sha} (resolved from
\texttt{huggingface\_hub.HfApi().model\_info(repo\_id).sha} at lock-in
time, 2026-05-03); the eval loop refuses to load a policy without one.
A single row is replayable end-to-end with
\texttt{python scripts/run\_one.py --policy P --env E --seed S}. The
Hub dataset (\texttt{thrmnn/lerobot-bench-v1}) is the data
source for the public Space and for every figure below. The full
sweep took \todo{N hours} of GPU time on the reference hardware.

\paragraph{Headline: SmolVLA on LIBERO-10 at task~0 measures 0.252
under our default protocol.}
SmolVLA's pooled rate across the four LIBERO suites is
0.621~[0.591, 0.651] under our protocol (\texttt{task\_id=0},
5~seeds $\times$ 50~episodes, env-default step caps). The per-suite
breakdown shows a sharp gradient: 0.928~[0.889, 0.954] on
\texttt{libero\_goal} and 0.776~[0.720, 0.823] on
\texttt{libero\_spatial}, against 0.528~[0.466, 0.589] on
\texttt{libero\_object} and 0.252~[0.202, 0.309] on
\texttt{libero\_10}. Published SmolVLA-0.45B
numbers~\cite{shukor2025smolvla} are suite averages over
10~tasks $\times$ 10~trials per task (0.90/0.96/0.92/0.71 for the
same four suites): ours is a single-task probe at \texttt{task\_id=0},
not an apples-to-apples replication, and the \texttt{libero\_10}
number is additionally a lower bound at our 520-step cap (74.8\% of
failed episodes truncated; canonical LIBERO uses 600). The
within-protocol measurement is tight (Wilson half-width $0.054$ at
$N{=}250$); the apples-to-apples claim defers to v1.1
(Section~\ref{sec:results-audit}, Future work).

\paragraph{SmolVLA${\times}$libero\_object shows a deterministic
ceiling.} On \texttt{libero\_object}, the across-seed variance of the
seed-level success rates is $1{\times}10^{-4}$ --- the five seeds
agree on $\hat{p}{=}0.528$ to within a single episode. The other
SmolVLA${\times}$LIBERO cells show variance two to forty times
higher (e.g.\ $5.9{\times}10^{-3}$ on \texttt{libero\_spatial},
$2.7{\times}10^{-3}$ on \texttt{libero\_10}). The
\texttt{libero\_object} failure mode is therefore consistent across
seeds rather than stochastic, suggesting a specific subset of object
configurations the checkpoint cannot solve regardless of the rollout
randomness --- a structural cap, not a sample-size problem. This is
the kind of signal the failure-mode taxonomy
(Figure~\ref{fig:taxonomy}) is meant to localise.

\paragraph{Baseline sanity.}
The no-op baseline succeeds zero times across all six envs
($N{=}1500$, Wilson upper bound $0.003$). The uniform-random
baseline succeeds zero times on \texttt{pusht} and on every LIBERO
suite, and is the only baseline above floor on
\texttt{aloha\_transfer\_cube} ($\hat{p}{=}0.052$,
[0.031, 0.087]) where the \texttt{gym-aloha} cube-transfer success
heuristic is the most permissive in the matrix. The baseline floor
is therefore not a single number across the leaderboard, which is
exactly why we run it on every cell rather than reporting it once.
For ACT on \texttt{aloha\_transfer\_cube} we measure
$\hat{p}{=}0.016$ [0.005, 0.038] --- below the
\texttt{aloha\_transfer\_cube} random floor --- but at the Hub-default
inference settings (\texttt{temporal\_ensemble\_coeff=None},
\texttt{n\_action\_steps=100}); the paper-canonical settings probe
is pending (see Methodology caveats below).

\paragraph{Methodology caveats (v1.0.1 audit).}
\label{sec:results-audit}
After v1.0 sweep completion we conducted a static methodology audit
against each policy's source paper and each env's canonical
protocol. Three mismatches were confirmed; all three constrain what
the headline cells mean without invalidating the underlying
measurements. (1)~\emph{Task coverage scope.} The SmolVLA
paper~\cite{shukor2025smolvla} Table~2 reports per-suite averages
over 10~tasks $\times$ 10~trials per task; v1 runs
\texttt{task\_id=0} $\times$ 5~seeds $\times$ 50~episodes per
suite. Every paper-vs-measured delta in Section~\ref{sec:results}
that cites a SmolVLA number is therefore a single-task envelope claim,
not a paper-replication claim
(PR~\#84, \texttt{docs/CLAIM\_AUDIT\_SMOLVLA.md}).
(2)~\emph{Inference settings: ACT temporal ensembling.} The pinned
\texttt{lerobot/act\_aloha\_sim\_transfer\_cube\_human} Hub config
defaults \texttt{temporal\_ensemble\_coeff=None},
\texttt{n\_action\_steps=100}; Zhao et al.~\cite{zhao2023learning}
Table~I used \texttt{coeff=0.01}, \texttt{n\_action\_steps=1}
(one action per step from a weighted mean over overlapping chunk
predictions). The 0.016 we measure on
\texttt{aloha\_transfer\_cube} is plausibly an inference-config
artefact rather than the architecture failing on the task; a
1-seed $\times$ 50-episode probe with paper settings is queued for
v1.0.2 and not yet complete (PR~\#86,
\texttt{docs/INFERENCE\_AUDIT.md}). (3)~\emph{Episode termination
caps on LIBERO.} The canonical LIBERO benchmark~\cite{liu2023libero}
uses \texttt{max\_steps=600} for all four suites; v1 inherited
lerobot's tighter caps \{280, 280, 300, 520\} (spatial, object,
goal, 10). Per-cell cap-hit rates on failed episodes are 22.4\%,
47.2\%, 7.2\%, and 74.8\% respectively, so all four LIBERO numbers
are lower bounds at our caps; the \texttt{libero\_10} headline cell
is the most sensitive to this. PR~\#90 implements a selectable
\texttt{--canonical} criterion (\texttt{docs/CANONICAL\_CRITERIA.md})
that bumps the cap to 600 and swaps PushT and Aloha success rules
to the paper-canonical sticky-true forms; v1.1 will rerun the
audit-affected cells under it. Neither the audit nor the patches
re-write any v1 parquet row: v1.0.0 numbers remain valid for the
single-task-at-truncated-cap scope they were measured under, and
the audit constrains the comparative claim, not the measurement.

\paragraph{XVLA deferral.}
The \texttt{xvla\_libero} policy was executed across all four LIBERO
suites (5~seeds${\times}$50 episodes on \{spatial, object, goal\},
5${\times}$25 on \texttt{libero\_10} after auto-downscope; 875~total
episodes) but is \textbf{deferred from the v1 leaderboard}. Two
distinct Hub-artifact wiring bugs --- a missing rotation-6D to
axis-angle post-processor and missing ImageNet normalization --- were
caught and patched in the loader (PR~\#71, PR~\#74), yet a third
unresolved issue still produced 0/40 in a post-patch sanity sweep
(Wilson upper bound $0.088$ at $N{=}40$, consistent with a complete
non-functional load rather than a partial regression). The deferral,
the two patched bugs, and the unresolved issue are documented in
\texttt{docs/DEFERRED\_POLICIES.md}; a complementary upstream issue
against \texttt{huggingface/lerobot} is in flight. XVLA returns to
the matrix in v1.1 once the third issue is identified and patched.

\paragraph{Stats sanity.}
Wilson and pivotal-bootstrap 95\% CIs agree to within one Wilson
half-width on every cell of the matrix (Methods,
Section~\ref{sec:methods}; notebook cell~11). No cell triggers the
disagreement flag. Across the 110 \textit{(policy, env, seed)} cells
in the sweep, the orchestrator advanced through 13 distinct
\texttt{code\_sha} values; the policy revision pins and the
\texttt{lerobot\_bench.stats} helpers were held constant across
those SHAs (only orchestrator and env-glue code drifted), which we
note as a Limitation rather than a confound: every parquet row
carries its originating SHA for downstream audit.

\paragraph{Failure-mode taxonomy.}
\label{sec:taxonomy}
We hand-label 5--10 failed rollouts per cell into six modes
(Figure~\ref{fig:taxonomy}; full definitions and labeling protocol in
\texttt{docs/FAILURE\_TAXONOMY.md}): \emph{trajectory overshoot}
(end-effector swings through the success region without reward),
\emph{gripper slip} (object lost from a closed gripper mid-trajectory
--- a physics outcome), \emph{timeout} (\texttt{truncated=True} with
the policy still issuing meaningful actions), \emph{wrong object}
(first interaction targets a non-success object; not applicable to
PushT), \emph{premature release} (\texttt{gripper.open=1} issued
outside the success region --- a control failure, distinct from
gripper slip), and \emph{drift} (action magnitude collapses or
oscillates with no net progress). Modes are not mutually exclusive;
each rollout is labeled with the first mode that fits in
chronological order, which keeps per-cell mode counts summing to the
total failure count and the bar chart legible. Failures are stratified
across seeds: if a cell has 5 seeds with 10 failures each, we draw
1~failure per seed, not 5 from one seed, so a single bad seed cannot
dominate the chart.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{What the leaderboard says.}
Three things, in order of confidence. First, the protocol works: every
parquet row is bit-replayable, Wilson and pivotal-bootstrap CIs agree
to within one half-width on every cell, and 107~cells dispatched with
zero failures over 25~hours unattended on commodity hardware. Second,
diffusion\_policy${\times}$pusht reproduces its Hub-card number to
within 1.4~pp (0.816 measured vs. 0.83 reported) at $N{=}125$ after
auto-downscope, suggesting the reproducibility floor on the matrix is
serviceable when policy and env both match their training protocol.
Third, the SmolVLA${\times}$\texttt{libero\_10} cell measures 0.252
under our default protocol and 0.71 in the paper --- but the v1.0.1
audit (Section~\ref{sec:results-audit}) has since bounded this gap by
two scope mismatches, so the right reading is ``this checkpoint at
task~0 and a 520-step cap is materially below the published 10-task
average,'' not ``the paper number does not replicate.'' The
distinction is load-bearing for what a reader takes away. The
contribution we stand behind without qualification is the protocol
itself: a single-PR contribution path for new policies, a shared seed
contract, and a CI-vs-MDE bookkeeping discipline that flags any
delta below the resolvability threshold as inconclusive rather than
as a finding. v1.1 closes the audit caveats and re-runs the affected
cells; v1 ships the protocol, the data, and the audit trail.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
    \item \textbf{Simulation only.} All numbers are sim. The
    sim-to-real gap is real and well-documented; lerobot-bench is a
    screening tool for sim performance, not a substitute for hardware
    evaluation. Real-world transfer is not measured; future work
    re-runs the matrix on hardware with the sim-to-real bridge of
    choice and reports the gap as its own table.
    \item \textbf{Single-hardware bias on wall-clock.} Per-step
    latency is from a single RTX~4060 laptop (8\,GB VRAM ceiling).
    Absolute ms/step on different GPUs will differ; the policy
    \emph{ranking} on a single GPU is what the auto-downscope rule
    consumes and that ranking is portable.
    \item \textbf{Host-RAM cold-load excludes the $\boldsymbol{\pi_0}$
    family in v1.} The $\pi_0$, $\pi_0$-FAST, and $\pi_{0.5}$
    checkpoints (PaliGemma-3B backbone) require approximately 30\,GB of
    host CPU RAM during cold load --- not from steady-state
    activations ($<\,4$\,GB peak VRAM), but from the
    \texttt{from\_pretrained} weight-conversion staging buffers under
    HuggingFace Transformers' default load path. On the 32\,GB WSL2
    laptop used for v1 calibration this exceeds the headroom available
    once other tenant workloads are running. We defer the $\pi_0$
    family to v1.1, where a quantized checkpoint (4- or 8-bit GPTQ) or
    \texttt{accelerate}'s \texttt{device\_map="auto"} streaming load
    is expected to bring cold-load RAM under 12\,GB. v1 therefore
    compares four learned policies, not the originally targeted seven.
    \item \textbf{Sparse matrix coverage.} The
    \textit{(policy, env)} grid is sparse by design: a cell is N/A
    only when no public Hub checkpoint exists, so cross-policy
    comparisons are valid only within shared envs. The single
    multi-policy block in v1 is the four LIBERO suites
    (\textit{spatial}, \textit{object}, \textit{goal}, \textit{10}),
    shared by SmolVLA and XVLA ($\pi_0$ family deferred as above);
    PushT and Aloha each carry one learned policy plus the two
    baselines.
    \item \textbf{Episode budget.} $N{=}250$ is chosen for the GPU
    budget --- not because it suffices to resolve every interesting
    delta. We document the MDE bound explicitly
    (Section~\ref{sec:methods}); $N{=}500$ would buy a $\sim 1.4\times$
    tightening, $N{=}50$ a $2.2\times$ widening. Headline claims
    cite only deltas above the per-cell MDE.
    \item \textbf{Multi-comparison concerns.} Any
    $(\text{policy}, \text{env})$ pair we did not pre-register as a
    planned comparison is exploratory; the headline measurement
    (SmolVLA${\times}$\texttt{libero\_10}) is a single planned
    paper-vs-measured comparison and clears the corresponding MDE
    band by an order of magnitude, but cross-policy paired tests on
    shared envs are deferred to v1.1 when XVLA returns to the matrix.
    \item \textbf{Protocol-vs-paper apples-to-apples.} Three
    audit-confirmed mismatches (Section~\ref{sec:results-audit},
    PRs~\#84/\#86/\#89) constrain what the SmolVLA and ACT
    paper-vs-measured cells mean: \texttt{task\_id=0} vs.\ 10-task
    suite averages, \texttt{temporal\_ensemble\_coeff=None} vs.\
    paper's 0.01, and LIBERO step caps tighter than canonical 600.
    The audit does not invalidate any v1 parquet row --- the
    measurements are valid for the scope they were taken under ---
    but it does mean per-cell deltas should be read as protocol-vs-paper
    rather than as direct replication failures. v1.1 ships
    (i)~all-10-task LIBERO coverage, (ii)~the ACT temporal-ensembling
    probe (probe pending in v1.0.2), and (iii)~the canonical 600-step
    cap via PR~\#90's selectable criterion.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future work}

Five paths. (1)~Close the v1.0.1 audit caveats
(Section~\ref{sec:results-audit}) by re-running the SmolVLA
${\times}$LIBERO and ACT${\times}$Aloha cells under PR~\#90's
\texttt{--canonical} criterion (600-step LIBERO cap, paper-canonical
PushT/Aloha success rules) and with the ACT temporal-ensembling
inference settings; this is the v1.0.2 / v1.1 unblocker for the
apples-to-apples paper-replication claim. (2)~Onboard the $\pi_0$
family ($\pi_0$, $\pi_0$-FAST, $\pi_{0.5}$) using quantized weights or
\texttt{accelerate} streaming load (see Limitations) --- this extends
the learned-policy axis from four to seven in v1.1. (3)~Add the
remaining LIBERO task variants (each suite has 10 tasks; v1 locks
\texttt{task\_id=0}). (4)~Onboard new policies as they appear on the
Hub --- the contribution path is a single PR adding a row to
\texttt{configs/policies.yaml}. (5)~Re-run the matrix on hardware
with the sim-to-real bridge of choice and report the gap as its own
table. The eval pipeline is structured for
upstream adoption as \texttt{lerobot.eval.multi\_seed}; a draft PR is
in flight at \todo{\texttt{huggingface/lerobot}\#TBD}.
