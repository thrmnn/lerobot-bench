\begin{abstract}
Reported success rates for pretrained LeRobot policies live in
self-reported tables across heterogeneous evaluation protocols, with
no shared seed accounting or confidence intervals. We introduce
\emph{lerobot-bench}, a public reproducible evaluation suite that runs
a fixed roster of pretrained policies on a fixed roster of LeRobot
sim envs under a multi-seed contract (5~seeds $\times$ 50~episodes per
\textit{(policy, env)} cell), reporting bootstrap 95\% confidence
intervals on every cell and paired comparisons across cells with
explicit minimum-detectable-effect bounds. The benchmark, accompanying
interactive HF Space, and the analysis notebook that produced every
figure in this paper are released as open data.
\todo{Headline finding sentence, one of: ``VLAs underperform classical
policies on PushT but dominate on Libero'' / ``no cross-policy delta
exceeds the MDE band at $N{=}250$, the leaderboard is the result.''
Choose the version the data supports; do not fabricate.}
The implication is that single-seed published numbers from individual
policy papers should not be compared head-to-head without re-running
under a shared protocol --- and the protocol we ship is one such.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

LeRobot~\cite{cadene2024lerobot} ships pretrained checkpoints for
Diffusion Policy~\cite{chi2023diffusion}, ACT~\cite{zhao2023learning},
SmolVLA, and Pi0 across simulated manipulation envs (PushT, Aloha,
Libero~\cite{liu2023libero}). Individual policy papers report
success rates on these envs, but the protocols differ in seed
counts, episode budgets, success thresholds, and how confidence
intervals (if any) are derived. The result is a set of numbers in
PDFs that nobody trusts head-to-head.

We fill that gap with \emph{lerobot-bench}: an open, reproducible,
multi-policy benchmark with three artifacts.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Open data.} A public HF Hub dataset
    (\texttt{thrmnn/lerobot-bench-results-v1}) of every per-episode
    outcome (success, return, step count, wall-clock, video SHA-256)
    plus an MP4 of every rollout, indexed by \textit{(policy, env,
    seed, episode)}.
    \item \textbf{Open eval.} A pinned, scriptable evaluation pipeline
    (\texttt{python scripts/run\_one.py --policy P --env E --seed S}) that
    re-derives any single parquet row in $<1$~h on commodity hardware,
    plus the resumable orchestrator that produced the full sweep.
    \item \textbf{Open analysis.} The notebook
    (\texttt{notebooks/01-write-finding.ipynb}) that produces every
    table and figure in this paper, including the bootstrap CIs, the
    paired-comparison effect sizes, and the failure-mode taxonomy.
\end{enumerate}

The contribution is methodological as much as empirical. We document
the seeding contract that makes the per-cell numbers bit-reproducible
at cell boundaries, the bootstrap protocol that makes the CIs
defensible at $N{=}250$ binary outcomes per cell, and the
minimum-detectable-effect bound that prevents over-claiming on
small deltas. The benchmark itself is the data; this paper is the
defense.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Sweep contract}

The unit of evaluation is a \textit{cell}: a tuple
$(\text{policy}, \text{env}, \text{seed\_idx})$ with
$\text{seed\_idx} \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$. Each cell runs $n_\text{ep}$
episodes; the contract is $n_\text{ep} = 50$ unless the auto-downscope
rule (Section~\ref{sec:downscope}) reduces it for a slow VLA. The
total per-cell sample size at the contract is
$N = 5 \times 50 = 250$ binary outcomes.

\paragraph{Episode boundary.} An episode runs from
\texttt{env.reset(seed=$s$)} until
\texttt{terminated=True}, \texttt{truncated=True}, or step count
equals env-specific \texttt{max\_steps} (PushT 300, Aloha 400,
Libero 600). Success is the per-env standard reward threshold from
\texttt{lerobot.envs.<env>.config.SUCCESS\_REWARD}; the episode
succeeds iff its final-step reward meets or exceeds it.

\subsection{Seeding contract}
\label{sec:seeding}

Per cell, at the start, three RNGs are seeded with the same value:
\begin{equation}
    \text{seed}_\text{cell} = \text{seed\_idx} \times 1000.
\end{equation}
Concretely: \texttt{numpy.random.seed}, \texttt{torch.manual\_seed},
and \texttt{torch.cuda.manual\_seed\_all} are all set to
$\text{seed}_\text{cell}$. Per-episode, the env is reset with
\begin{equation}
    \text{seed}_\text{ep}(e) = \text{seed}_\text{cell} + e,
    \quad e \in \{0, \ldots, n_\text{ep} - 1\}.
\end{equation}
Policy stochasticity inherits the torch generator and is \emph{not}
re-seeded per episode. The torch generator therefore advances across
episodes within a cell, which means mid-cell resume is \emph{not}
bit-reproducible: a cell that crashes mid-way must restart from
$e=0$. The orchestrator (\texttt{checkpointing.plan\_resume}) classifies
cells as \emph{completed} (full \texttt{set(range($n_\text{ep}$))} of
episode indices present), \emph{partial} (any other non-empty subset),
or \emph{pending} (no rows), and re-runs partial cells from scratch.

\subsection{Sample size and confidence intervals}

For each cell we report success rate
$\hat{p} = (\text{successes})/N$ with a percentile-bootstrap 95\% CI
over the flat list of $N$ binary outcomes. The bootstrap uses
$n_\text{resamples} = 10{,}000$ resamples drawn with a seeded
\texttt{numpy.random.Generator(42)}; this matches the
\texttt{lerobot\_bench.stats.bootstrap\_ci} implementation, which we
sanity-check against the closed-form Wilson score
interval~\cite{wilson1927probable} (the bootstrap interval converges
to Wilson at the per-cell sample sizes we run).

The unit of resampling is the \emph{episode}, not the seed.
Bootstrapping over the 5 seeds gives wide CIs and conflates between-seed
variance with the inferential question; the design explicitly fixes
the unit at the episode level so the per-cell intervals reflect
``how confident are we in this $\hat{p}$,'' not ``how often does seed
choice shift it.''

\subsection{Paired comparisons across cells}

For pairs of cells with identical $(\text{seed\_idx}, e)$ pairings ---
i.e.\ the same protocol on the same env --- we report a paired
$\Delta\hat{p}$ via two methods: a paired bootstrap that draws shared
indices into both arrays
(\texttt{stats.paired\_delta\_bootstrap}, same $n_\text{resamples}$
and seed as the marginal CI), and the two-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank
test on the per-episode outcome differences (\texttt{stats.paired\_wilcoxon},
\texttt{zero\_method=`wilcox'} drops zero-difference pairs as is
standard for binary paired data; this is the McNemar limit). Effect
size is Cohen's $h$~\cite{cohen1988statistical}:
\begin{equation}
    h = 2 \arcsin(\sqrt{p_1}) - 2 \arcsin(\sqrt{p_2}),
\end{equation}
with the conventional bands $|h| < 0.2$ small, $< 0.5$ medium,
$\ge 0.8$ large.

\paragraph{MDE bound.} At $N = 250$, the Wilson 95\% half-width at
the worst case $\hat{p} = 0.5$ is
$\text{HW} \approx 0.062$. Following~\cite{wilson1927probable}, any
$|\Delta\hat{p}| < 2 \cdot \text{HW} \approx 0.124$ is inconclusive
at this $N$ regardless of the bootstrap or Wilcoxon p-value: two
cells whose marginal CIs each have half-width up to 0.062 can produce
a sample delta below 0.124 just from sampling noise. Every paired
comparison in Section~\ref{sec:results} is annotated with whether
its delta clears this threshold; deltas below it are reported as
\emph{inconclusive at $N{=}250$} rather than as findings.

\subsection{Auto-downscope rule}
\label{sec:downscope}

Total per-cell wall-clock budget is 3~hours on a single RTX~4060
laptop GPU (8\,GB VRAM). After Day~0 calibration produces per-policy
$\overline{t}$~ms/step, the per-cell episode count is
\begin{equation}
    n_\text{ep} = \min\!\left(50, \left\lfloor
        \frac{3 \times 3600}{5 \cdot \texttt{env.max\_steps} \cdot
        \overline{t} / 1000} \right\rfloor\right).
\end{equation}
If a policy's per-cell minimum (5~episodes/seed $\times$ 5~seeds = 25
episodes) still exceeds budget, the policy is dropped from v1, not
silently truncated. Any cell with $n_\text{ep} < 50$ is flagged in the
leaderboard and in this paper's table caption.

\subsection{Sparse matrix policy}

A \textit{(policy, env)} cell is N/A only when no public HF Hub
checkpoint exists at lock-in time. Sparsity is reported in
Section~\ref{sec:results}, not silently skipped. Cross-policy
comparisons are made only within shared envs.

\subsection{Reproducibility}

Each parquet row carries the code commit SHA, the lerobot version
pin (\texttt{lerobot==0.5.1}), and the SHA-256 of its rendered MP4.
A single row is replayable end-to-end with
\texttt{python scripts/run\_one.py --policy P --env E --seed S}. The
HF Hub dataset (\texttt{thrmnn/lerobot-bench-results-v1}) is the data
source for the public Space (\texttt{huggingface.co/spaces/thrmnn/lerobot-bench})
and for every figure below. The full sweep took

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Simulation only.} All numbers are sim. The sim-to-real
    gap is real and well-documented; lerobot-bench is a screening
    tool for sim performance, not a substitute for hardware
    evaluation.
    \item \textbf{Single-hardware bias on wall-clock.} Per-step latency
    figures are from a single RTX~4060 laptop; absolute ms/step on
    different GPUs will differ, although the ranking across policies
    on the same hardware is what the auto-downscope rule consumes
    and that ranking is portable.
    \item \textbf{Sparse matrix coverage.} The
    \textit{(policy, env)} grid is sparse by design --- a cell is N/A
    only when no public HF Hub checkpoint exists. Cross-policy
    comparisons are valid only within shared envs.
    \item \textbf{Multi-comparison concerns.} Any
    $(\text{policy}, \text{env})$ pair we did not pre-register as
    a planned comparison should be read as exploratory; we report
    them all in Table~\ref{tab:paired} for transparency but the
    abstract's headline finding cites only the pre-registered pair.
    \item \textbf{Fine-tune track is single-cell.} The
    fine-tune comparison (\todo{add if shipped}) lifts (or fails to
    lift) one worst-case cell with 100~demos and LoRA rank~16. This
    is a smoke test on data scale, not an end-to-end replication of
    any one policy's training recipe.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future work}

Three paths. (1)~Drop the 4-hour Libero install cap and add the
remaining Libero task variants once the install is automated.
(2)~Onboard new policies as they appear on the Hub --- the
contribution path is one PR adding a row to
\texttt{configs/policies.yaml}. (3)~Re-run on hardware with the
sim-to-real bridge of choice and report the gap as its own table.

\subsection{Upstream PR}

The eval pipeline is structured for upstream adoption as
\texttt{lerobot.eval.multi\_seed}; a draft PR is in flight at
\todo{\texttt{huggingface/lerobot}\#TBD}.
