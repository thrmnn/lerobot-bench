\begin{abstract}
The animating question of this work is whether a zero-training world-model
planner can stand in for a learned policy, and where it cannot: does its
success track the dynamical complexity of the environment? We answer it at
two endpoints --- zero-training world-model latent-MPC planning solves
\emph{navigation} but not \emph{contact}, replicated across two independent
world-model families --- but answering even those endpoints cleanly
requires a ruler that scores incompatible paradigms together, and
\emph{building that ruler is the load-bearing contribution of this paper};
the full curve across the gradient middle is not a result we claim here.
Robot-policy success rates are reported per
paradigm --- pretrained imitation, fine-tuning, classical control,
world-model planning --- on incompatible protocols, so the paradigms
cannot be put on one axis. We introduce \emph{embodimetry}, a
\emph{Capability-Ladder Audit}, and stand behind three
reproducible contributions. \emph{First, a single cross-paradigm
evaluation contract}: pretrained, fine-tuned, classical-control, and
world-model-planner policies are all scored as the \emph{same}
\texttt{PolicyCallable} under one seed / Wilson-and-bootstrap-CI /
minimum-detectable-effect (MDE) ruler on shared
LeRobot~\cite{cadene2024lerobot} tasks, with floor baselines and honest
negatives (a SmolVLA-LoRA collapse, a classical controller at $0.012$)
kept on the board --- no neighboring effort puts paradigms on one ruler
with the negatives retained. \emph{Second, a self-auditing methodology}:
the instrument caught and corrected its \emph{own} headline overclaim ---
a normalization bug in our harness pinned ACT on Aloha-transfer-cube at
$\hat{p}{=}0.016$ (below the env's random floor), and a clean $2{\times}2$
ablation isolated the full $0.016\to0.824$ recovery to the normalization
fix, not inference settings --- and a guard test code-enforces that the
over-claimed top rung stays labeled to its evidence. Our claim is that an
eval suite must be audited as carefully as the policies it scores. The
ladder is the honest-negatives spine that earns the contract credibility.
\emph{Third, a strengthened-but-qualified L3 reading the contract was built
to test:} zero-training world-model latent-MPC planning solves
\emph{navigation} but not \emph{contact}, and this \emph{replicates across
two independent world-model families} (DINO-WM and
\texttt{facebookresearch/jepa-wms}) under matched receding-horizon MPC ---
Wall navigation JEPA-WM $4/6 \approx$ DINO-WM $3/6$, PushT quasi-static
contact JEPA-WM $0/6 \approx$ DINO-WM $0/6$. We report this as a
strengthened result, not a cross-env law: it is a \emph{two-endpoint}
contrast at $N{=}6$ episodes per cell with one environment per endpoint, so
the gradient \emph{middle} is unmeasured (a MetaWorld anchor is future
work). \textbf{L0}
(pretrained Diffusion Policy on PushT) scores $0.816$~[0.739, 0.874].
\textbf{L1} (continued fine-tuning of an already-converged ACT on
Aloha-transfer-cube) moves $0.824$~[0.772, 0.866] to $0.864$~[0.816,
0.901] --- a $+0.040$ shift whose CIs \emph{overlap} (Cohen's $h{=}0.11$,
below the $N{=}250$ MDE), reported as within noise; and an attempted
SmolVLA LoRA fine-tune collapses ($0.252\to{\sim}0$). \textbf{L2} (a
competent scripted PushT controller) clears the strict bar on only
$0.012$~[0.004, 0.035] of episodes despite mean rollout coverage near
$0.50$. \textbf{L3} is the strengthened-but-qualified rung: under matched
receding-horizon MPC (re-plan every step), zero-training world-model
planning solves Wall navigation (JEPA-WM $4/6 \approx$ DINO-WM $3/6$) but
not PushT contact (JEPA-WM $0/6 \approx$ DINO-WM $0/6$), and the
navigation-not-contact split \emph{replicates across two independent
world-model families}. The earlier one-shot $0/6$ JEPA-WM Wall reading was
a \emph{planning-protocol artifact} (full-horizon one-shot vs.\
receding-horizon), corrected by a protocol control; the contact endpoint
stays ${\sim}0$ \emph{even with} receding-horizon, so the nav$>$contact gap
is neither a protocol artifact nor DINO-WM-checkpoint-specific. We hold this
as a strengthened result with explicit caveats, not a cross-env law:
$N{=}6$ episodes per cell (small), \emph{one} environment per endpoint, so
this is a two-endpoint contrast and the gradient \emph{middle} is
unmeasured (a MetaWorld middle anchor is future work). The L3 cells run as a
cross-repo artifact (\texttt{lerobot-wm-research}) on the same eval
contract. The contributions we stand behind are the contract, the
self-audit, and this two-endpoint nav-vs-contact replication; the full
dynamics-complexity \emph{curve} remains future work.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The question that animates this work is whether a zero-training world-model
planner can stand in for a learned policy, and \emph{where} it cannot:
does its success track the dynamical complexity of the environment, holding
at point-mass navigation but breaking at contact? Answering even the two
endpoints cleanly requires scoring a world-model planner and a learned
policy on \emph{one} ruler, and \textbf{building that ruler is the
load-bearing contribution of this paper}; the two-endpoint nav-vs-contact
reading it produces (\S\ref{sec:l3}) is a strengthened result, but the full
\emph{curve} across the dynamics-complexity gradient is not something we
claim here. We stand behind three reproducible contributions
(\S\ref{sec:l3}). \emph{(1) A single cross-paradigm evaluation contract.}
Robot control is pursued along a ladder of paradigms with increasing
autonomy and decreasing supervision: \textbf{L0} zero-shot evaluation of
a pretrained policy, \textbf{L1} fine-tuning that policy on a small demo
budget, \textbf{L2} hand-engineered classical control, and \textbf{L3}
planning through a learned world model. Each rung has its own literature
and its own way of reporting success, so a Diffusion Policy
number~\cite{chi2023diffusion}, an ACT fine-tuning
number~\cite{zhao2023learning}, a scripted-controller baseline, and a
world-model planner~\cite{zhou2024dinowm,assran2025vjepa2} are never
measured on a common axis. The protocols differ in seed counts, episode
budgets, success thresholds, and whether confidence intervals are
reported at all --- a reproducibility gap documented repeatedly in
adjacent fields~\cite{henderson2018deep,agarwal2021deep}. embodimetry
scores all four paradigms as the same \texttt{PolicyCallable} under one
seed / Wilson-and-bootstrap-CI / MDE ruler, with floor baselines and
honest negatives (the SmolVLA-LoRA collapse, the classical $0.012$) kept
on the board --- no neighbor (HF leaderboards, MLPerf, Open
X-Embodiment~\cite{bohg2025open}, or the world-model
benchmarks~\cite{zhou2024dinowm,assran2025vjepa2}) puts paradigms on one
ruler with the negatives retained. \emph{(2) A self-auditing methodology.}
The instrument caught and corrected its own headline overclaim, code-fenced
against re-inflation by a guard test (\S\ref{sec:methods}); we develop this
below as the load-bearing claim that an eval suite must be audited as
carefully as the policies it scores.

\emph{embodimetry} is a \emph{Capability-Ladder Audit}: it measures
multiple paradigms as the \emph{same} \texttt{PolicyCallable}
(\texttt{obs} $\to$ \texttt{action}) through \emph{one} seed /
Wilson-CI / MDE contract on shared LeRobot~\cite{cadene2024lerobot}
tasks. A pretrained Diffusion Policy, a fine-tuned ACT, and a scripted
PushT controller all enter the same eval loop, are seeded identically,
and are scored with the same Wilson interval, so their numbers sit on
one yardstick instead of in four PDFs. The instrument is paradigm-neutral
by construction: it has no stake in which rung wins.

We release embodimetry across three surfaces: open data (a public
Hugging Face Hub~\cite{huggingfacehub2024} dataset of every per-episode
outcome plus an MP4 of every rollout); open eval (a pinned, scriptable
pipeline that re-derives any parquet row in $<1$\,h on commodity
hardware); and open analysis (the notebook
\texttt{notebooks/01-write-finding.ipynb} that produces every table and
figure here). The v1 leaderboard is four learned policies and two
weights-free floor baselines crossed with six envs (22~runnable cells
after the checkpoint-availability filter); the ladder rungs (L1, L2) are
gated \emph{out} of that leaderboard and run as separate audit cells on
the identical contract.

The result we lead with is that the instrument audits itself. The
contract flagged a normalization bug in \emph{our own} eval harness ---
ACT on \texttt{aloha\_transfer\_cube} read $\hat{p}{=}0.016$, below the
env's random floor, until we traced it to un-normalized image
observations; a clean $2{\times}2$ ablation pins the entire
$0.016\to0.824$ recovery on the normalization fix, with temporal
ensembling a wash (\S\ref{sec:results-audit}). A loss curve would never
have surfaced this; only a closed-loop success rate with a confidence
interval did. That is the load-bearing claim of the paper: the value of a
neutral contract is that it bites on the surface it is supposed to
protect, including the auditor's own.

This paper reports all four rungs on the same contract. The L0--L2 rungs
are a spine of honest negatives that is what makes the contract
trustworthy: fine-tuning a near-ceiling policy moves the number only
\emph{within noise} (L1, ACT); an attempted SmolVLA LoRA fine-tune
collapses rather than lifts; and a competent classical controller clears
the strict success bar on ${\sim}1\%$ of episodes despite getting halfway
there on average (L2) --- a quantitative case that the value of learning
is the last fraction of precision.

At the top of the ladder we report a \emph{strengthened-but-qualified}
result. Under matched receding-horizon MPC (re-plan every step), a
zero-training world-model latent-MPC planner --- no task-specific training
--- solves \emph{navigation} but not \emph{contact}, and this
nav-not-contact split \emph{replicates across two independent world-model
families}, DINO-WM~\cite{zhou2024dinowm} and
\texttt{facebookresearch/jepa-wms}~\cite{assran2025vjepa2}. On \textbf{Wall},
two-room point-mass navigation, the planner reaches $4/6$ (JEPA-WM)
$\approx 3/6$ (DINO-WM); on \textbf{PushT}, quasi-static contact
manipulation, it scores $0/6$ (JEPA-WM) $\approx 0/6$ (DINO-WM), at
${\sim}1000\times$ the per-step compute of the learned diffusion policy that
scores $0.816$ on the same env. Two confounds the earlier reading carried
are now controlled: an apparent $0/6$ JEPA-WM Wall failure was a
\emph{planning-protocol artifact} (full-horizon one-shot vs.\
receding-horizon), corrected by a protocol control, and the
nav-vs-contact direction no longer rests on a \emph{single} world-model
family, because it replicates across DINO-WM and \texttt{jepa-wms}.
Crucially, the contact endpoint stays ${\sim}0$ \emph{even with}
receding-horizon and \emph{across both families}, so the nav$>$contact gap
is neither a protocol artifact nor DINO-WM-checkpoint-specific
(\S\ref{sec:l3}). The qualification we keep in the same breath: $N{=}6$
episodes per cell is small, and there is \emph{one} environment per endpoint,
so this is a \emph{two-endpoint} contrast --- the gradient \emph{middle}
(reach, multi-contact) is unmeasured and a MetaWorld middle anchor is future
work --- not a full curve and not a cross-env law. The contract is what made
this replication measurable on one shared seed/CI protocol. The benchmark
itself is the data; this paper is the defense.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Robot-learning benchmarks and statistical rigor.}
PushT~\cite{chi2023diffusion}, the Aloha suite~\cite{zhao2023learning},
and LIBERO~\cite{liu2023libero} are the de facto LeRobot references;
RLBench~\cite{james2020rlbench}, CALVIN~\cite{mees2022calvin}, and Open
X-Embodiment~\cite{bohg2025open} offer larger taxonomies but no
pretrained LeRobot-compatible checkpoints or shared protocol. None ship a
multi-policy leaderboard with shared seed accounting, multi-seed CIs, and
re-runnable rows. We adopt the multi-seed, bootstrap-CI, MDE-bounded
posture that Henderson et al.~\cite{henderson2018deep} and Agarwal et
al.~\cite{agarwal2021deep} established for deep RL, and add weights-free
no-op/uniform-random floor baselines on every cell (per the
Gym~\cite{brockman2016openai} convention) since some envs admit non-zero
random success.

\paragraph{Cross-paradigm and world-model planning.}
The ladder we audit spans paradigms each benchmarked in isolation:
classical control and learned policies are compared only informally, and
world-model planners --- PlaNet~\cite{hafner2019planet},
DINO-WM~\cite{zhou2024dinowm}, V-JEPA-2-AC~\cite{assran2025vjepa2} ---
report success on their own tasks, never against a learned imitation
policy under one shared seed/CI protocol. Our contribution is not a new
policy class but the \emph{contract} that puts a pretrained, fine-tuned,
classical, \emph{and} world-model planner on the same Wilson interval ---
with floor baselines and honest negatives retained, which no neighboring
benchmark does --- plus the \emph{cross-env, cross-family study it makes
possible}: running the same receding-horizon latent-MPC loop across two
world-model families (DINO-WM, \texttt{jepa-wms}) on envs of differing
dynamical complexity, which yields a two-endpoint reading that
zero-training planning solves navigation but not contact, replicated across
both families (\S\ref{sec:l3}). That two-endpoint contrast is a
strengthened result; the full curve across the gradient middle is not yet
measured.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\paragraph{Sweep contract.}
The unit of evaluation is a \textit{cell}:
$(\text{policy}, \text{env}, \text{seed\_idx})$ with
$\text{seed\_idx} \in \{0,\ldots,4\}$. Each cell runs $n_\text{ep}$
episodes; the contract is $n_\text{ep}{=}50$ unless the auto-downscope
rule (below) reduces it, giving $N = 5 \cdot 50 = 250$ binary outcomes.
An episode runs from \texttt{env.reset(seed=$s$)} until
\texttt{terminated}, \texttt{truncated}, or step count reaches
env-specific \texttt{max\_steps} (PushT 300, Aloha 400, LIBERO
$\{280,280,300,520\}$ for spatial/object/goal/10, inherited from
lerobot defaults; canonical LIBERO uses 600 for all four, an audit
caveat in Section~\ref{sec:results-audit}). Success is the per-env
\texttt{SUCCESS\_REWARD} threshold; the episode succeeds iff its
final-step reward meets or exceeds it. PR~\#90 adds a selectable
\texttt{--canonical} criterion (paper termination rule, 600-step LIBERO
cap); v1 ships the lerobot-default rule and v1.1 reruns affected cells.

\paragraph{Seeding contract.}
Per cell, three RNGs are seeded with
$\text{seed}_\text{cell} = \text{seed\_idx} \cdot 1000$
(\texttt{numpy}, \texttt{torch}, \texttt{torch.cuda}). Per episode the
env is reset with $\text{seed}_\text{ep}(e) = \text{seed}_\text{cell}+e$.
Policy stochasticity inherits the torch generator and is \emph{not}
re-seeded per episode, so the generator advances across episodes within
a cell: mid-cell resume is \emph{not} bit-reproducible and a crashed
cell restarts from $e{=}0$. The orchestrator
(\texttt{checkpointing.plan\_resume}) classifies cells as completed,
partial, or pending and re-runs partial cells from scratch.

\paragraph{Confidence intervals and paired comparisons.}
Each cell reports $\hat{p}{=}\text{successes}/N$ with two mutually
sanity-checked 95\% CIs: the closed-form Wilson score
interval~\cite{wilson1927probable,agresti1998approximate} (shown in the
tables) and a percentile bootstrap~\cite{efron1979bootstrap,efron1993introduction}
($10{,}000$ resamples, seeded \texttt{Generator(42)}, resampling over
\emph{episodes} not seeds); the two agree within 1\,pp at $N{=}250$. For
two cells sharing $(\text{seed\_idx}, e)$ pairings we report a paired
$\Delta\hat{p}$ with a pivotal bootstrap CI and the Wilcoxon
signed-rank test~\cite{wilcoxon1945individual} (reducing to
McNemar~\cite{mcnemar1947note} for binary outcomes), plus Cohen's
$h{=}2\arcsin\!\sqrt{p_1}-2\arcsin\!\sqrt{p_2}$~\cite{cohen1988statistical}
($|h|{<}0.2$ small). Multiple within-env ranking claims are controlled by
Holm--Bonferroni~\cite{bonferroni1936teoria} at $\alpha{=}0.05$.

\paragraph{Minimum detectable effect.}
At $N{=}250$ the Wilson 95\% half-width at worst case $\hat{p}{=}0.5$ is
$\text{HW}=0.0615$; any $|\Delta\hat{p}| < 2\cdot\text{HW} = 0.1230$ is
inconclusive at this $N$ regardless of $p$-value, since two cells with
half-widths up to 0.0615 can produce a smaller sample delta from
sampling noise alone. The threshold shrinks for saturated/near-zero
cells ($0.0543$ at $\hat{p}{=}0.95$); \texttt{docs/MDE\_TABLE.md}
tabulates the per-$\hat{p}$ threshold and the empirical paired bootstrap
MDE at $\rho\in\{0,0.3,0.5\}$. Every paired comparison in
Section~\ref{sec:results} is annotated against this threshold; deltas
below it are reported as \emph{inconclusive at $N{=}250$}, not as
findings.

\paragraph{Auto-downscope rule.}
\label{sec:downscope}
The per-cell wall-clock budget is 3~hours on a single RTX~4060 laptop
GPU (8\,GB VRAM). Given a per-policy mean step latency $\overline{t}$
(ms/step) from Day-0 calibration, the episodes-per-seed count is
\begin{equation}
    n_\text{ep} = \min\!\left(50,\ \left\lfloor
        \frac{3 \cdot 3600}{5 \cdot \texttt{env.max\_steps} \cdot
        \overline{t} / 1000} \right\rfloor\right).
\end{equation}
Seed count is fixed at five; only $n_\text{ep}$ shrinks, so a
down-scoped cell lands at $N\in\{50,100\}$ with a wider CI flagged in
the caption. Two v1 cells were down-scoped. If the per-cell
minimum ($N{=}50$) still exceeds budget, the policy is dropped from v1,
not silently truncated.

\paragraph{Sparse matrix and reproducibility.}
A cell is N/A only when no public Hub checkpoint exists at lock-in,
reducing the $6\times6$ grid to 22~runnable cells; sparsity is a visible
gap, not silently skipped. Each parquet row carries its code SHA, the
lerobot pin (\texttt{0.5.1}), and the SHA-256 of its MP4; every policy is
locked to a fixed \texttt{revision\_sha} and the eval loop refuses to load
one without it. A single row replays via \texttt{run\_one.py}. The full
sweep took $\approx 25$\,h of GPU time on the reference hardware
(1$\times$ RTX 4060 Laptop, WSL2).

\paragraph{The same contract across rungs.}
\label{sec:ladder-contract}
Every rung enters the audit as a \texttt{PolicyCallable}: a function
mapping an observation dict to an action, with no knowledge of which
paradigm produced it. The L1 fine-tuned ACT, the L2 scripted controller,
and the L0 pretrained checkpoints all run through the \emph{identical}
\texttt{embodimetry.eval.run\_cell} loop --- the same per-cell seeding
contract, the same $5{\times}50$ budget, and the same env-canonical
success rule the corresponding zero-shot cell used. The only thing that
varies between an L0 and L1 cell is the policy weights; between an L0 and
L2 cell, the policy is weights-free scripted control. Ladder cells are
gated out of \texttt{V1\_POLICIES} (the published leaderboard roster) and
written to a separate \texttt{results/ladder/} tree, so an audit rung can
never leak into the leaderboard or rewrite a canonical parquet row, while
still being directly CI-comparable to it.

\paragraph{L1 rung --- continued fine-tuning.}
We full-fine-tune the pretrained ACT checkpoint on a small demo budget
from \texttt{aloha\_transfer\_cube} and re-measure on the contract its
zero-shot cell used (canonical \texttt{sticky\_reward\_eq}, reward${=}4$,
$N{=}250$). ACT (${\sim}50$\,M params) full-FT peaks at ${\sim}2.8$\,GB
VRAM and fits the 8\,GB budget; SmolVLA full-FT does not, so L1 is a
single-cell ACT probe (see Limitations). Fine-tuning an already-converged
policy on its own data is expected to yield a near-zero lift; we report
the lift with its paired effect size and call it within noise when its CI
straddles zero, rather than dropping the negative result.

\paragraph{L2 rung --- classical-control quality.}
The L2 cell runs a deterministic scripted state-feedback PushT controller
through the same loop under the strict canonical PushT rule
(\texttt{sticky\_is\_success}, $\text{coverage}>0.95$). Because a binary
success rate alone hides \emph{how close} a competent controller gets, we
additionally log the per-episode max-coverage
$\max_t \text{info}[\text{coverage}]_t$ (summary JSON only, never the
parquet schema): the gap between mean max-coverage and the strict $0.95$
bar quantifies the last-mile precision that the binary rate discards.

\paragraph{L3 rung --- zero-training world-model planning.}
The L3 cell is a \emph{zero-training} planner: a frozen latent forward
model wrapped in a \emph{receding-horizon} cross-entropy-method (CEM) MPC
loop, entering the audit as a \texttt{PolicyCallable} with no task-specific
training and its per-step sampling cost logged alongside its success rate.
At each step the planner encodes the current observation once, rolls
candidate action sequences through the latent model, and \emph{re-plans}
--- it executes only the first action of the lowest-cost sequence (cost
${=}$ latent distance to the encoded goal) and re-optimizes from the next
observation, rather than committing to a full-horizon one-shot plan. We run
this loop with \emph{two independent world-model families} ---
DINO-WM~\cite{zhou2024dinowm} and
\texttt{facebookresearch/jepa-wms}~\cite{assran2025vjepa2} --- on two envs
of differing dynamical complexity: \textbf{Wall}, a two-room point-mass
navigation task, and \textbf{PushT}, quasi-static contact manipulation. The
receding-horizon protocol is itself a \emph{control}: an earlier $0/6$
JEPA-WM Wall reading came from a full-horizon \emph{one-shot} plan, and
switching to matched receding-horizon recovers it to $4/6$, isolating that
$0$ as a planning-protocol artifact rather than a model failure. The CEM
budget is reduced to fit an 8\,GB GPU; a $39\times$ predictor speedup
(scaled-dot-product attention, \texttt{bfloat16}, encode-once frame
caching) is what made the loop feasible on this hardware, and the budget is
below the H100-scale CEM the source methods use, which we flag as a
magnitude (not direction) caveat. These L3 cells live in a separate
research repository (\texttt{lerobot-wm-research}); we report them as a
\emph{cross-repo} artifact run on the same eval contract, and mark their
figures' provenance accordingly rather than fabricating an in-tree
artifact. Matching the planning protocol and replicating across two
model families controls the protocol and single-family confounds the
earlier reading carried; the two cells still differ in env (and the
per-env checkpoint that env requires), so this is a \emph{two-endpoint}
contrast, not a swept curve (\S\ref{sec:l3}).

\paragraph{Anti-inflation guard (the self-audit, code-enforced).}
\label{sec:guard}
The self-auditing methodology is not just a posture, it is a test.
\texttt{tests/test\_no\_ungated\_l3\_claims.py} runs in CI and \emph{fails
the build} if any public surface (this paper, the README, the living
leaderboard, the deck, the blog) \emph{over-claims} the world-model rung.
It permits the strengthened two-endpoint reading (navigation solved,
contact not, replicated across two model families) but requires that
reading to keep its caveats: it forbids the specific triumphalist tokens
the original audit flagged, fails the build if the paper claims a full
cross-env \emph{law} or a measured dynamics-complexity \emph{curve}, and
fails if the two-endpoint qualifiers (the $N{=}6$ per-cell sample, the
single environment per endpoint) are dropped from the paper. It also
asserts the $0.016\to0.824$ self-caught-bug lead is present on every
surface. The guard is citable evidence of the self-audit: it makes the
qualified status of the L3 reading a \emph{property of the repository}, not
an editorial promise, so the result cannot be silently inflated from a
two-endpoint contrast into a cross-env law between releases.

\paragraph{L0 / L1 --- pretrained and fine-tuned, side by side.}
The pretrained Diffusion Policy reads $\hat{p}{=}0.816$~[0.739, 0.874] on
PushT. On the L1 cell, fine-tuning the already-converged ACT checkpoint on
its own \texttt{aloha\_transfer\_cube} demos moves the success rate from
$0.824$~[0.772, 0.866] ($206/250$) to $0.864$~[0.816, 0.901] ($216/250$).
The point estimate rises $+0.040$, but the two Wilson intervals overlap
on $[0.816, 0.866]$, the paired effect size is small (Cohen's
$h{=}0.11$), and $|\Delta\hat{p}|{=}0.040$ is below the $N{=}250$ MDE of
$0.123$ (\S\ref{sec:downscope}). We therefore report L1 as a
\emph{within-noise, non-significant shift}, not a fine-tuning win. This
is the expected and honest outcome of continued fine-tuning a policy
already near its ceiling on its own training distribution; the negative
result is the finding, and it is kept on the leaderboard's axis rather
than dropped. We do not silently drop the second fine-tune track either:
an attempted SmolVLA LoRA fine-tune \emph{collapsed} the policy
($0.252\to{\sim}0$ on \texttt{libero\_10}\todo{confirm exact post-LoRA
$\hat{p}$ and $N$ from the fine-tune track summary; not committed to this
tree}) rather than lifting it --- a negative we attribute to a small demo
budget against a large adapter and an unfavorable reward structure,
reported as a failed lift, not omitted.

\paragraph{L2 --- a competent controller, and the last 50\% of precision.}
The scripted PushT state-feedback controller is genuinely competent ---
its mean per-episode max-coverage is $0.505$ (median $0.501$),
i.e.\ on an average rollout it pushes the block roughly
halfway to the target footprint --- yet it
clears the strict canonical bar ($\text{coverage}>0.95$) on only
$0.012$~[0.004, 0.035] of episodes ($3/250$). Read against the L0
learned-policy rate on the same env, this is a quantitative statement of
where the value of learning lives: not in getting started, but in the
last fraction of precision a hand-tuned controller cannot close. The
mean-coverage figure is logged in the L2 summary JSON; the binary
$0.012$ is the contract-comparable number.

\subsection{L3 --- navigation solved, contact not: a two-endpoint
replication}
\label{sec:l3}
This rung is a \emph{strengthened-but-qualified} result. Under matched
receding-horizon MPC (re-plan every step), a zero-training world-model
latent-MPC planner solves \emph{navigation} but not \emph{contact}, and the
split \emph{replicates across two independent world-model families} ---
DINO-WM~\cite{zhou2024dinowm} and
\texttt{facebookresearch/jepa-wms}~\cite{assran2025vjepa2}
(Table~\ref{tab:l3}). On \textbf{Wall}, two-room point-mass navigation, the
planner reaches $4/6$ (JEPA-WM) $\approx 3/6$ (DINO-WM). On \textbf{PushT},
quasi-static contact manipulation, it scores $0/6$ (JEPA-WM) $\approx 0/6$
(DINO-WM): it moves the block but pushes it away from the goal, at
${\sim}1000\times$ the per-step compute of the learned diffusion policy
that scores $0.816$ on the same env. Two confounds the earlier reading
carried are now controlled. \emph{(1) Planning protocol.} An earlier $0/6$
JEPA-WM Wall reading came from a full-horizon \emph{one-shot} plan; matching
the receding-horizon protocol DINO-WM used recovers it to $4/6$, isolating
that $0$ as a planning-protocol artifact, not a model failure. \emph{(2)
Single-family.} The nav-vs-contact direction no longer rests on one
world-model family: it replicates across DINO-WM and \texttt{jepa-wms}.
Critically, the contact endpoint stays ${\sim}0$ \emph{even with}
receding-horizon and \emph{across both families}, so the nav$>$contact gap
is neither a protocol artifact nor DINO-WM-checkpoint-specific. The
qualifiers we keep in the same breath: $N{=}6$ episodes per cell is small,
and there is \emph{one} environment per endpoint (Wall for navigation, PushT
for contact), so this is a \emph{two-endpoint} contrast --- the gradient
\emph{middle} (e.g.\ reach, multi-contact) is unmeasured and a MetaWorld
middle anchor is future work. We therefore report a strengthened
two-endpoint nav-vs-contact replication, not a measured dynamics-complexity
\emph{curve} and not a cross-env \emph{law}.

\paragraph{Proof the audit bites: a normalization bug we caught in our
own eval.}
A secondary but load-bearing result --- evidence the audit machinery
actually catches errors rather than laundering them --- is a bug in
\emph{our own} harness. ACT's apparent failure on
\texttt{aloha\_transfer\_cube} ($\hat{p}{=}0.016$~[0.006, 0.040], below
the env's random floor) turned out to be ours, not the architecture's. An early
build of the eval loop silently failed to apply dataset normalization stats
to \texttt{observation.images.top}, feeding ACT un-normalized image
observations; PR~\#51 fixed it
(\texttt{\_recover\_dataset\_stats\_from\_safetensors} now disambiguates
buffer names against the config's \texttt{feature\_keys}). After the fix the
same checkpoint reads the canonical \textbf{0.824~[0.772, 0.866]}
(Table~\ref{tab:leaderboard}). To attribute the recovery cleanly we ran a
controlled $2{\times}2$ ablation crossing normalization \{buggy, fixed\}
with inference \{Hub-default, paper-settings\}, each cell $N{=}250$
(Table~\ref{tab:act-ablation}): the recovery is 100\% the normalization
fix, and \emph{temporal ensembling is a wash, not the cause} (broken
normalization stays at 0.016 under either inference setting; once fixed,
Hub-default and paper settings overlap in Wilson CI). Figure~\ref{fig:replication}
plots every cell's measured rate against its paper-reported rate and shows
the $0.016\to0.824$ jump directly. The lesson
generalises: an eval suite must be audited --- normalization,
buffer wiring, observation plumbing --- as carefully as the policies it
scores (Section~\ref{sec:results-audit}).

\paragraph{Methodology caveats (v1.0.1 audit).}
\label{sec:results-audit}
A static audit surfaced three mismatches that constrain comparative
claims without invalidating any measurement. (1)~\emph{Task scope:} every
SmolVLA cell is \texttt{task\_id=0}, so paper-vs-measured deltas are
single-task envelopes, not replications. (2)~\emph{ACT normalization bug}
(self-caught, PR~\#51): covered above and in Table~\ref{tab:act-ablation};
the $0.016\to0.824$ recovery is entirely the norm fix, temporal ensembling
a wash. (3)~\emph{LIBERO step caps:} v1 inherited lerobot caps shorter than
canonical LIBERO's 600~\cite{liu2023libero}; a cap=600 probe on
\texttt{libero\_10} reads 0.256~[0.206, 0.314] vs.\ 0.252 (within
half-width, policy- not cap-bottlenecked), other suites pending v1.1. No
patch rewrites a parquet row. We rely on label-free failure signals
(episode-length and cap-hit distributions, plus every failed-episode MP4)
rather than a labeled taxonomy, which is specified but deferred to v1.1.
XVLA ran on LIBERO but is deferred from v1 over an unresolved
Hub-artifact wiring bug (\texttt{docs/DEFERRED\_POLICIES.md}).

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{What the ladder says.}
Three things stand. Two are settled, citable contributions --- the
cross-paradigm contract and the self-auditing methodology --- and the
third is a strengthened-but-qualified L3 reading the contract was
purpose-built to produce. The self-audit claim is narrow and defensible: a
neutral seed/Wilson-CI/MDE contract surfaced a normalization bug that
pinned ACT$\times$aloha at $0.016$ (below the random floor) and a clean
$2{\times}2$ ablation attributed the full $0.016\to0.824$ recovery to the
fix (Table~\ref{tab:act-ablation}) --- an error a loss curve hides and only
a closed-loop CI exposes --- and a guard test (\S\ref{sec:guard})
code-enforces that the rung above keeps its caveats. At the top of the
ladder we report that, under matched receding-horizon MPC, a zero-training
world-model planner solves \emph{navigation} but not \emph{contact}, and
this split \emph{replicates across two independent world-model families}
(Wall JEPA-WM $4/6 \approx$ DINO-WM $3/6$; PushT $0/6 \approx 0/6$). Two
confounds the earlier reading carried are now controlled --- the planning
protocol (an earlier one-shot $0/6$ Wall was a protocol artifact, recovered
to $4/6$ by matched receding-horizon) and the single-family limit (the
split replicates across DINO-WM and \texttt{jepa-wms}) --- and the contact
endpoint stays ${\sim}0$ even with receding-horizon and across both
families, so the nav$>$contact gap is robust to both. We hold this as a
strengthened result with its caveats kept in view: $N{=}6$ per cell, one
environment per endpoint, so it is a \emph{two-endpoint} contrast, not a
measured curve and not a cross-env law --- the gradient middle is future
work.

The supporting spine is a set of honest negatives that earn the audit its
credibility. First, the contract works as a cross-paradigm yardstick:
pretrained, fine-tuned, classical, and world-model policies ran through
one loop, were seeded identically, and are read off the same Wilson
interval (Tables~\ref{tab:ladder},~\ref{tab:l3}); every L0--L2 row is
bit-replayable and Wilson/bootstrap CIs agree within one half-width.
Second, the measured rungs say something specific and undramatic:
fine-tuning a near-ceiling policy moves it within noise ($+0.040$,
overlapping CIs, $h{=}0.11$) and an attempted SmolVLA LoRA fine-tune
collapses rather than lifts, so neither L1 track is inflated into a win;
and a competent classical controller gets halfway to the goal on average
but clears the strict bar on ${\sim}1\%$ of episodes ($\hat{p}{=}0.012$),
locating the value of learning in the last fraction of precision. Third,
the machinery catches its own errors: ACT$\times$aloha's $0.016\to0.824$
recovery (Table~\ref{tab:act-ablation}) was a normalization bug in the
harness, not the architecture. The two contributions we stand behind
without qualification are (i)~the \emph{contract} --- one
seed/Wilson-CI/MDE spine from pretrained policies to world-model planners,
with floor baselines and honest negatives retained, sub-MDE deltas flagged
inconclusive, and overlapping CIs called overlapping --- and (ii)~the
\emph{self-audit} --- the instrument correcting its own $0.016\to0.824$
overclaim, with a guard test (\S\ref{sec:guard}) code-enforcing that the
top rung keeps its caveats. The third, qualified contribution is the L3
two-endpoint replication: under matched receding-horizon MPC, zero-training
world-model planning solves navigation but not contact, replicated across
DINO-WM and \texttt{jepa-wms} (Wall $4/6 \approx 3/6$; PushT $0/6 \approx
0/6$) --- a strengthened result at $N{=}6$ per cell with one environment per
endpoint, so a two-endpoint contrast, not a cross-env law; the full
dynamics-complexity curve (with the MetaWorld middle anchor) is future
work.

\subsection{Why the two endpoints behave differently, and what would
turn them into a curve}
The two-endpoint reading is strengthened but it is still two endpoints, and
it is worth stating cleanly why they differ and what would complete the
curve. The intuition: Wall's point-mass dynamics are low-dimensional,
near-linear, and contact-free, so a frozen latent forward model predicts
them accurately enough that receding-horizon CEM finds a goal-reaching plan
it was never trained on --- and this holds for \emph{both} world-model
families, which is why it reads as a property of the dynamics, not of one
checkpoint. PushT's dynamics are contact-rich and stiff, so small latent
errors compound through contact and the planner pushes the block
confidently the wrong way --- again for both families, which is why the
contact failure is robust rather than a single-checkpoint artifact. So
``do we need a learned policy?'' already looks, at the two endpoints, like a
question about the modellability of the dynamics. What our evidence
\emph{cannot} yet establish is the shape \emph{between} the endpoints: each
cell is $N{=}6$, and there is one environment per endpoint, so the middle of
the gradient (reach, multi-contact, deformable) is unmeasured. What would
turn the two endpoints into a measured curve is the study the contract
enables: the \emph{same} receding-horizon planner at the \emph{same} budget
across a \emph{sequence} of envs (point-nav $\to$ reach $\to$ contact $\to$
deformable, with a MetaWorld middle anchor), each at a seed count whose
Wilson CI clears chance, with the planner's compute cost
(${\sim}1000\times$ per step) reported alongside. Until then the defensible
statement is exactly the two-endpoint one: zero-training world-model
planning solves navigation but not contact, replicated across two
world-model families --- two points, not yet a curve.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}\setlength{\parskip}{0pt}\setlength{\topsep}{0pt}\setlength{\partopsep}{0pt}
    \item \textbf{Simulation only.} All numbers are sim; the sim-to-real
    gap is unmeasured. embodimetry is a screening tool, not a
    substitute for hardware evaluation.
    \item \textbf{Single-hardware wall-clock.} Per-step latency is from
    one RTX~4060 laptop; absolute ms/step differs across GPUs, but the
    \emph{ranking} the auto-downscope rule consumes is portable.
    \item \textbf{$\boldsymbol{\pi_0}$ family deferred.} $\pi_0$,
    $\pi_0$-FAST, $\pi_{0.5}$ (PaliGemma-3B) need ${\approx}30$\,GB host
    RAM during \texttt{from\_pretrained} cold load ($<4$\,GB peak VRAM),
    exceeding the 32\,GB v1 laptop's headroom; v1 compares four learned
    policies, not seven (v1.1 uses quantized/streaming load).
    \item \textbf{L3 is a strengthened two-endpoint result, not a curve and
    not a cross-env law.} The nav-vs-contact split is real and replicates
    across two world-model families (DINO-WM, \texttt{jepa-wms}) under
    matched receding-horizon MPC --- the protocol and single-family
    confounds the earlier reading carried are now controlled. But two
    limitations remain and we state them plainly. \emph{(i) Small $N$:}
    each cell is $N{=}6$ episodes, so the per-cell rates ($3/6$, $4/6$,
    $0/6$) are coarse. \emph{(ii) One environment per endpoint:} navigation
    is a single env (Wall) and contact is a single env (PushT), so the
    contrast is \emph{two endpoints}, not a swept curve --- the gradient
    \emph{middle} (reach, multi-contact, deformable) is unmeasured, and a
    MetaWorld middle anchor is future work. The two cells still differ in
    the per-env checkpoint each env requires, but holding the planning
    protocol fixed and replicating across two model families removes the
    protocol and single-family explanations for the gap. The CEM budget is
    reduced to fit 8\,GB, below the source methods' H100-scale budgets --- a
    magnitude, not direction, caveat. The L3 cells are also a
    \emph{cross-repo} artifact (\texttt{lerobot-wm-research}), not an
    in-tree parquet of this repository.
    \item \textbf{Single-GPU compute ceiling.} The entire audit runs on
    one 8\,GB RTX~4060 laptop. This caps the ladder beyond L3: SmolVLA
    full fine-tune does not fit, so L1 is a single ACT cell plus an
    attempted SmolVLA LoRA track that collapsed ($0.252\to{\sim}0$),
    reported as a failed lift rather than dropped.
    \item \textbf{L1 is one cell, and the lift is within noise.} The
    fine-tuning rung is ACT$\times$aloha alone, and its $+0.040$ shift has
    overlapping Wilson CIs and $h{=}0.11$ (below the $N{=}250$ MDE). We
    cannot claim fine-tuning helps from this cell; we can only show the
    contract reads a near-ceiling continued-FT as the non-result it is. A
    multi-cell L1 sweep with a larger demo budget and a from-scratch
    baseline is needed to say anything stronger.
    \item \textbf{SmolVLA single-task scope.} Every SmolVLA cell is
    \texttt{task\_id=0} only; the paper~\cite{shukor2025smolvla} averages
    10~tasks $\times$ 10~trials. Our SmolVLA numbers are single-task
    envelopes, not suite replications, and the cross-paradigm comparison
    should not lean on them.
    \item \textbf{Sparse matrix, multi-comparison, and episode budget.}
    Cross-policy comparisons are valid only within shared envs; the
    L0/L1/L2 rungs sit on \emph{different} envs (PushT, aloha), so they are
    same-contract but not same-task and must be read as paradigm
    snapshots, not a head-to-head. The L3 two-endpoint result is the exception
    that motivates the design: it holds the \emph{planning protocol} fixed,
    replicates across two model families, and varies the \emph{env}, which
    is the cleanest cross-env comparison the contract supports --- but it is
    only two envs (one per endpoint), so navigation-vs-contact is a
    two-endpoint contrast, not a curve. With several pairwise claims in
    play we control family-wise error by Holm--Bonferroni, but the matrix
    is too sparse for a fully crossed comparison. $N{=}250$ is a
    GPU-budget choice; claims cite only deltas above the per-cell MDE.
    \item \textbf{Harness bugs and protocol-vs-paper.} Our own
    normalization bug (PR~\#51) shows a harness can be the dominant error
    source; the LIBERO cap mismatches make those cells protocol-vs-paper,
    not replication failures. Cross-policy paired tests defer to v1.1 when
    XVLA returns.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Future work.}
Turn the two L3 endpoints into a measured curve: run the \emph{same}
receding-horizon planner at a larger seed count and a fuller
(paper-faithful) CEM budget on a GPU that affords it, holding the planner
and budget fixed while varying \emph{only} the env across the gradient
middle (point-nav $\to$ reach $\to$ contact $\to$ deformable, with a
MetaWorld middle anchor), so the two-endpoint nav-vs-contact reading becomes
a swept curve at a seed count whose Wilson CIs clear chance; the two-family
replication (DINO-WM, \texttt{jepa-wms} with
V-JEPA-2-AC~\cite{assran2025vjepa2}) already holds at the endpoints and
extends to the middle envs. Broaden L1 to a
multi-cell, larger-budget fine-tuning sweep with a from-scratch baseline
so the rung can support a positive claim, and diagnose the SmolVLA LoRA
collapse. Close the audit caveats (cap=600 LIBERO reruns); onboard the
$\pi_0$ family via quantized/streaming load; and re-run on hardware for
the sim-to-real gap.
